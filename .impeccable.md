## Design Context

### Users

Volta serves a broad public audience in Portugal returning beverage containers for deposit refunds. Users span children to elderly adults and include many levels of digital literacy. They typically use the app in physical, time-sensitive environments such as supermarkets or public collection points, often while carrying multiple items and interacting with a machine. The interface must be understandable at first glance and support universal comprehension without requiring prior familiarity.

The core job is to complete a quick, reliable deposit-return transaction: identify the user, return valid containers, avoid rejection errors, and receive money. Users experience this primarily as a financial and operational task with immediate feedback, not as a sustainability workflow.

### Brand Personality

Volta should feel practical, transparent, and close. The tone should be clear, reassuring, efficient, and trustworthy, with a slight encouraging quality that never becomes moralizing. Communication should stay direct and human, avoiding bureaucratic, technocratic, or overly instructional language.

### Aesthetic Direction

The product should prioritize light mode because primary use happens in bright retail environments and because the brand’s core visual system centers on cyan in clean, high-contrast layouts. The existing cyan (`#0CC3D7`) is the primary identity color and should stay dominant, supported by white, black, and light gray neutrals. Avoid green-heavy palettes and avoid introducing new dominant brand colors.

Useful references:

- Apple Wallet for fast scanning, low-friction flows, and immediate confirmation.
- Uber for clear state transitions and constant reassurance through feedback.
- MB Way for familiar QR and payment patterns, trust in money-related actions, and simple confirmations.

Anti-references:

- Government or bureaucratic interfaces with dense forms and heavy instructions.
- Greenwashed or overly eco-coded visuals.
- Complex, feature-heavy dashboards.
- Any experience that feels slow, unclear, or cognitively demanding.

### Design Principles

1. Optimize for instant comprehension in real-world use. Every core screen should be understandable within seconds, even for users with low digital literacy.
2. Treat every key flow as a money and transaction experience. Prioritize trust, confirmation, and operational clarity over lifestyle or sustainability signaling.
3. Minimize reading and friction. Prefer simple wording, strong icon support, obvious next actions, and compact flows over explanation-heavy interfaces.
4. Make feedback immediate and explicit. Every action should produce a clear system response, especially scans, errors, validation states, and payout-related events.
5. Design for inclusive clarity. Use large legible type, strong contrast, non-color status cues, and actionable error messages that help users recover fast.
